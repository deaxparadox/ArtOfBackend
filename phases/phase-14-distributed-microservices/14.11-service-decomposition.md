## 1. What Is It? (Theory)

The hardest problem in microservices is not the technical implementation — it's deciding **where to draw the service boundaries**. Wrong boundaries create tight coupling between services, which defeats the purpose of microservices entirely.

Domain-Driven Design (DDD) provides a systematic approach to finding natural boundaries through **bounded contexts** — explicit areas of the domain where a model is internally consistent and has a clear, defined language.

---

## 2. The Bounded Context Concept

```
THE "PRODUCT" PROBLEM:

In an e-commerce company, "Product" means different things to different teams:

CATALOG TEAM:
  Product = {name, description, images, category, specifications}
  Care about: search optimization, content quality, SEO

INVENTORY TEAM:
  Product = {sku, warehouse_location, stock_level, reorder_point}
  Care about: availability, stock tracking, procurement

PRICING TEAM:
  Product = {base_price, discounts, regional_pricing, cost}
  Care about: margins, promotions, competitive pricing

FULFILLMENT TEAM:
  Product = {dimensions, weight, packaging_requirements, handling_notes}
  Care about: shipping cost, packing efficiency

These are FOUR DIFFERENT MODELS of "Product."
They share a name but have different attributes, different behaviors, different owners.

BOUNDED CONTEXT INSIGHT:
  Don't try to create one universal "Product" model.
  Instead: recognize that "Product" means something different in each context.
  
  Catalog Context: their Product model, their database, their team
  Inventory Context: their Product model (more like a SKU), their database, their team
  Pricing Context: their PricedItem model, their database, their team
  Fulfillment Context: their ShipItem model, their database, their team
  
  Each context is internally consistent.
  Across contexts: explicit translation/mapping at the boundary.
```

## 3. How to Find Bounded Contexts

```
TECHNIQUES:

1. EVENT STORMING (Alberto Brandolini):
   Workshop with domain experts + engineers.
   Map all domain events on a timeline:
   "User browsed catalog" → "User added to cart" → "User checked out" → "Payment processed" → ...
   
   Natural clustering of events reveals domain boundaries.
   Events owned by different teams = different services.

2. LINGUISTIC BOUNDARIES:
   Where does the ubiquitous language change?
   
   When the Sales team says "Account" and the Finance team says "Account":
   Do they mean the same thing? Probably not.
   Sales: Account = a customer company with contacts and opportunities
   Finance: Account = a financial ledger account with debits and credits
   
   Different meaning = different bounded context.

3. TEAM OWNERSHIP:
   Conway's Law: your architecture mirrors your org structure.
   Identify natural team ownership boundaries.
   Each team owns one or a few bounded contexts.
   Service boundaries = team boundaries.

4. CHANGE FREQUENCY:
   Parts that change together, deploy together.
   Parts that change independently, deploy independently.
   
   Pricing changes daily → separate pricing service
   Product descriptions change weekly → catalog service
   Core order processing rarely changes → keep in monolith

5. SCALING REQUIREMENTS:
   Parts that scale independently → separate service.
   Search: scales with traffic → separate
   Order management: scales with GMV → separate
   Admin: scales with team size → probably monolith
```

## 4. Service Decomposition Patterns

```
BY BUSINESS CAPABILITY (recommended for most systems):
  Each service owns one business capability end-to-end.
  
  OrderService: everything about placing and tracking orders
  PaymentService: everything about money movement
  CatalogService: everything about products
  NotificationService: everything about customer communications
  
  Services are stable even when business processes change.
  "Add a new payment method" → change PaymentService only.

BY SUBDOMAIN (DDD-aligned):
  Core subdomains: competitive differentiators → own microservice
  Supporting subdomains: important but not differentiating → possibly shared or outsourced
  Generic subdomains: commodity → use off-the-shelf solutions
  
  For an e-commerce company:
  Core: recommendation engine, dynamic pricing → own microservice, top talent
  Supporting: order management, inventory → own microservice, standard team
  Generic: email sending, PDF generation → use SendGrid, DocRaptor (SaaS)

BY DATA ISOLATION:
  Each service owns its data exclusively.
  No service can directly access another service's database.
  
  If two services must share data: expose it via an API.
  This is what forces proper service boundaries.
```

---

## 5. Senior Engineer Perspective

**The service boundary is the hardest decision:**

A service boundary is much harder to change than code within a service. Once two services are in production with clients, changing the boundary (merging them or splitting them differently) requires:
- Coordinating all clients of both services
- Data migration
- Potential downtime

Get boundaries wrong, and you end up with a distributed monolith: microservices that are so tightly coupled they must be deployed together anyway.

**The bounded context test:**

Can you change the internal data model of Service A without any code changes in Service B? If not, the boundary is in the wrong place. Service B is depending on Service A's internal structure, not just its interface.
