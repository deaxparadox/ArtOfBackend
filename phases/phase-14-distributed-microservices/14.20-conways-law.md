## 1. Conway's Law (14.20)

### What Is It?

**Conway's Law (Mel Conway, 1967):**

> "Organizations which design systems are constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of these organizations."

In plain English: your software architecture will mirror your team structure. Teams that communicate through formal processes build systems with formal interfaces. Teams that communicate informally build systems with loose coupling.

This is not a prescriptive rule — it's an observation about human nature and organizational behavior.

### The Inverse Conway Maneuver

If Conway's Law says "architecture copies org structure," the Inverse Conway Maneuver says: **design your org structure to get the architecture you want**.

```
TRADITIONAL: build the architecture first, org structure follows
  "Let's build microservices" → team reorganizes around services
  → Services often reflect team boundaries rather than domain boundaries
  → Services have wrong granularity (too fine-grained or too coarse)

INVERSE CONWAY: design org first, architecture follows naturally
  "We want Services A, B, C with these boundaries" 
  → Form teams A, B, C with clear ownership
  → Each team naturally builds the service they own
  → Services match domain boundaries because teams match domains

EXAMPLE:
  
  WRONG: 5 teams (frontend, backend, infra, data, ops) build an e-commerce system.
  → They produce a 5-tier architecture: presentation, API, business logic, data, ops
  → No domain boundaries. Every feature touches all 5 tiers.
  → Adding a "checkout" feature requires coordinating all 5 teams.
  
  RIGHT: Teams organized by domain (catalog, orders, payments, fulfillment, identity).
  → Each team produces a service for their domain.
  → Adding checkout: mostly the orders team, with APIs to payments and inventory.
  → Other teams: minimal coordination.
```

### Practical Implications

```
ORGANIZATIONAL ANTI-PATTERNS THAT CREATE BAD ARCHITECTURES:

ANTI-PATTERN 1: "Horizontal" teams (frontend, backend, DB admin)
  → Creates "horizontal" architecture with many dependencies per feature
  → Every user story crosses team boundaries (slow, coordinated deploys)
  
ANTI-PATTERN 2: One team per microservice regardless of size
  → A "payment" team of 2 engineers owns the payment service
  → A "recommendation" team of 8 engineers owns the recommendation service
  → Neither team is right-sized for their service
  
ANTI-PATTERN 3: One shared services team
  → Auth, logging, configuration all owned by one "platform" team
  → Every other team blocked waiting for platform team
  → Platform team becomes the bottleneck for everything

BETTER PATTERNS:
  Domain teams: 5-8 engineers, own 1-3 services in their domain
  Platform team: internal developer tooling (deploy pipelines, observability)
  Enabling team: temporary team to help domain teams adopt new capabilities
```

## 2. When NOT to Use Microservices (14.21)

This topic is as important as knowing when to use them.

```
CLEAR SIGNALS THAT MICROSERVICES ARE WRONG:

1. SMALL TEAM (< 10 engineers):
   Microservices double or triple operational overhead.
   Your team spends 50% on infrastructure instead of product.
   Wrong trade-off: value operational simplicity over theoretical scale.

2. STARTUP / UNVALIDATED DOMAIN:
   You don't yet know the right service boundaries.
   Premature microservices lock you into wrong boundaries.
   When the domain evolves: you face painful service merges or splits.
   
   "Make it work, then make it clean, then make it fast" — premature splitting
   makes it very hard to "make it clean" later.

3. NO STRONG OBSERVABILITY FOUNDATION:
   Microservices debugging requires: distributed tracing, centralized logging,
   service-level dashboards, alerting per service.
   Without this: debugging a production issue is a nightmare.
   Don't go microservices without observability infrastructure.

4. NO CI/CD PER SERVICE:
   Microservices require independent deployment pipelines per service.
   If you're manually deploying, microservices multiply your manual work.
   Automate everything first.

5. STRONG TRANSACTIONALITY REQUIREMENTS:
   If your core business logic requires ACID transactions across many entities:
   - Saga complexity may be worse than a monolith
   - The domain might truly belong in one service
   - Re-examine service boundaries before accepting the distributed transaction complexity

6. PREMATURE OPTIMIZATION:
   "We might need to scale X independently" → theoretical scaling concern
   Build for actual scale when you have actual scale.
   Premature microservices for scaling you don't need = unnecessary complexity.

7. THE DISTRIBUTED MONOLITH:
   The worst outcome: microservices that must be deployed together.
   If service A and service B always change together, they should be one service.
   If they cannot be deployed independently due to data coupling: they're a distributed monolith.
   All the costs of microservices, none of the benefits.
```

### The Migration Path

```
IF YOU HAVE A MONOLITH AND WANT MICROSERVICES:

DON'T:
  Big-bang rewrite. High risk. Long time. Unclear benefit at each step.

DO:
  Strangler Fig Pattern:
  1. Identify a specific component with clear, concrete needs for extraction
     (specific scaling need, specific team ownership, specific tech requirement)
  2. Extract ONLY that component
  3. Route traffic to the new service
  4. Retire the monolith component
  5. Operate the new service. Learn from it. Fix issues.
  6. Repeat for the next component (when you have the next concrete need)
  
  This produces:
  - Incremental de-risking (monolith still works during migration)
  - Learning from each extraction before the next
  - Only extract when there's a concrete reason
  
IF YOU HAVE MICROSERVICES THAT ARE TOO FINE-GRAINED:
  
  Identify services that:
  - Always deploy together
  - Share the same database anyway
  - Are owned by the same team
  - Require distributed transactions between them
  
  → Merge them into one service.
  It's embarrassing to merge services, but it's the right call.
  Amazon Prime Video merged microservices into a monolith and saved 90% on costs.
```

## 3. Senior Engineer Perspective

**The courage to not use microservices:**

In 2024, there's a counter-narrative forming against the microservices-by-default culture. The Amazon Prime Video post, Basecamp's "The Majestic Monolith," Shopify's well-structured Rails monolith handling billions in GMV — these are credible, successful examples of monolith-first or monolith-forever strategies.

The senior engineer's job is to ask: "What problem am I solving?" before adopting any architectural pattern. Microservices solve: organizational scale, independent deployment, independent scaling, technology diversity. If you don't have these problems, you probably don't need microservices.

**Conway's Law as a planning tool:**

Before your next architecture decision, draw your org chart. Then draw the system you're planning to build. If they don't match: either change the architecture or change the team structure. If they're in conflict: the org structure will win. You can't maintain an architecture that fights your organization.
