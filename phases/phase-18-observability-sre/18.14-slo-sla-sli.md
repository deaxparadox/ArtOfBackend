## 1. The Vocabulary of Reliability (Theory)

Three terms from Google's SRE book that define how reliability is measured and managed:

```
SLI (Service Level Indicator):
  A quantitative measure of service behavior.
  The actual metric you measure.
  
  Examples:
  - Request success rate: (successful requests) / (total requests)
  - Latency: p99 request duration
  - Availability: fraction of time service returns healthy responses

SLO (Service Level Objective):
  A target value for an SLI. What you're trying to achieve.
  Internal commitment between engineering and the business.
  
  Examples:
  - Request success rate ≥ 99.9% (measured over rolling 30 days)
  - p99 latency ≤ 300ms (measured over rolling 7 days)
  - API availability ≥ 99.95%

SLA (Service Level Agreement):
  A contractual promise to customers.
  Usually less aggressive than your internal SLO.
  Violating an SLA has financial/legal consequences.
  
  Examples:
  - "We guarantee 99.9% uptime or you get 10% service credit"
  - "p99 response time < 1 second or service credit applies"

THE RELATIONSHIP:
  SLI: what you measure
  SLO: what you target internally
  SLA: what you promise externally (with consequences for violation)
  
  SLO is always tighter than SLA:
  SLO: 99.95% availability (internal target)
  SLA: 99.9% availability (customer commitment)
  Gap allows you to breach the SLO without immediately violating the SLA.
```

## 2. Designing Good SLIs

Not every metric makes a good SLI. Good SLIs directly measure the user experience.

```
GOOD SLIs (user-centric):
  ✓ Request success rate (did the user's request succeed?)
  ✓ Latency at user-facing endpoints (did it respond fast enough?)
  ✓ Checkout completion rate (did users complete the purchase?)
  
  These directly measure whether users are getting the service they expect.

BAD SLIs (system-centric):
  ✗ CPU utilization (doesn't directly indicate user experience)
  ✗ Database connection pool utilization (internal signal, not user-visible)
  ✗ Deployment frequency (doesn't measure reliability)
  
  These are useful for debugging but not for SLOs.

AVAILABILITY SLI:
  availability = count(successful_requests) / count(all_requests)
  
  "Successful" = HTTP 2xx and 3xx (not 5xx)
  Often exclude: health check endpoints, intentional 4xx (user errors)
  
  Prometheus query:
  1 - (
    sum(rate(http_requests_total{status_code=~"5.."}[5m]))
    /
    sum(rate(http_requests_total[5m]))
  )

LATENCY SLI:
  "What fraction of requests complete within X ms?"
  
  latency_sli = count(requests <= 300ms) / count(all_requests)
  
  Prometheus query:
  sum(rate(request_duration_seconds_bucket{le="0.3"}[5m]))
  /
  sum(rate(request_duration_seconds_count[5m]))
```

## 3. Setting SLO Targets

```
HOW TO CHOOSE YOUR SLO TARGET:

STEP 1: Measure your current baseline
  "What's our actual availability over the last 30 days?"
  Measure it. Don't guess.

STEP 2: Consider user impact
  99% availability = 7.2 hours of downtime per month
  99.9%           = 43 minutes
  99.99%          = 4.3 minutes
  99.999%         = 26 seconds
  
  Is 43 minutes of downtime per month acceptable to your users?
  If they're doctors checking patient records: probably not.
  If they're reading a blog: probably yes.

STEP 3: Consider the cost
  Each 9 of reliability costs exponentially more:
  99%   → one load balancer, decent database
  99.9% → redundant everything, multi-AZ
  99.99% → multi-region, complex failover, large SRE team

STEP 4: Set achievable targets
  Bad: "We'll aim for 99.999% because we care about users"
  Good: "Our current baseline is 99.95%. We'll target 99.9% with 0.05% buffer."
  
  Aspirational SLOs that your system can't actually meet breed cynicism.
  Start with your current performance minus a small buffer.

EXAMPLE SLO DOCUMENT:
  Service: Payment API
  
  SLO 1: Availability
    SLI: fraction of requests returning 2xx/3xx
    Target: ≥ 99.9%
    Window: rolling 30 days
    Exclude: known maintenance windows (announced 24h in advance)
  
  SLO 2: Latency
    SLI: fraction of requests completing within 500ms
    Target: ≥ 99.0%
    Window: rolling 7 days
  
  SLO 3: Success Rate for Critical Paths
    SLI: fraction of checkout flows completing without error
    Target: ≥ 99.5%
    Window: rolling 30 days
```

## 4. SLO-Based Alerting (The Right Way to Alert)

Traditional alerting: alert when a metric crosses a threshold.  
Problem: too noisy, too many false positives.

SLO-based alerting: alert when you're burning through your error budget faster than planned.

```
MULTI-WINDOW MULTI-BURN-RATE ALERTING:

Error budget: 0.1% of requests can fail (for 99.9% SLO)
30 days = 2,592,000 seconds × 0.001 = 2,592 seconds of error budget

BURN RATE CONCEPT:
  Burn rate 1 = consuming budget at exactly the rate that depletes
                it at the end of the window
  Burn rate 5 = consuming budget 5x too fast (will exhaust in 6 days)
  Burn rate 14.4 = will exhaust in 2 days (critical!)

ALERTING RULES:
  
  CRITICAL (page immediately):
    Short window: 5% of budget gone in 1 hour
    This means: burn rate > 14.4 (exhausts in 2 days)
    
    AND
    
    Long window: 5% of budget gone in 6 hours
    (Short window prevents false positives from brief spikes)
  
  WARNING (ticket, handle next business day):
    Short window: 10% of budget gone in 6 hours
    Burn rate > 6 (exhausts in 5 days)

# Prometheus alerting rules for SLO:
groups:
  - name: slo_alerts
    rules:
      # CRITICAL: burning too fast in short and long window
      - alert: PaymentAPIHighBurnRate
        expr: |
          (
            rate(http_requests_total{service="payment",status_code=~"5.."}[1h])
            / rate(http_requests_total{service="payment"}[1h])
          ) > (14.4 * 0.001)    # 14.4x burn rate
          AND
          (
            rate(http_requests_total{service="payment",status_code=~"5.."}[6h])
            / rate(http_requests_total{service="payment"}[6h])
          ) > (14.4 * 0.001)
        for: 2m
        labels:
          severity: critical
        annotations:
          summary: "Payment API burning SLO budget critically fast"
```

## 5. Senior Engineer Perspective

**SLOs change the conversation between engineering and business:**

Without SLOs, reliability discussions are vague: "Is the service reliable?" "We think so, mostly." With SLOs, the discussion is concrete: "We're at 99.91% for the month against our 99.9% target. We used 90% of our error budget in the last 3 days due to the database incident."

This creates alignment: the business understands what they're getting, and engineering has a quantitative framework for deciding when reliability work is more important than feature work. If error budget is exhausted, no feature deployments until reliability is restored.

**99.9% is the right target for most web services:**

Many teams instinctively target "five nines" (99.999%). That's 26 seconds of downtime per month. Achieving it requires massive investment in redundancy, failover automation, and operational discipline. For most SaaS products, 99.9% (43 minutes/month) is more than sufficient. Save the five nines for services where downtime has serious safety or financial consequences.
